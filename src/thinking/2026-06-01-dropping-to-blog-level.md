title: 'Dropping to blog level'
description: 'In which I re-learn the need to balance writing and building.'
tags: ["ai", "software"]
---

It's been quiet around here. Too quiet.

I haven't published much in the past three months. I haven't [read much](/bookshelf) during that time either. Instead, I've spent most of that time building.

As the entire software industry grapples with what the hell it means to have machines that can now crank out code faster and at higher quality than most humans, I've been doing what I always do when the pundits proclaim some new transformational epoch: rolling up my sleeves, ignoring the hype, and learning by doing. In that regard, these few months have proven extraordinarily fruitful: I've generated a lot of code. I've explored the current edges of LLMs as they pertain to software engineering practices. And by now I like to think I have a good idea of where these things succeed, where they fail, what models are capable of what, what harnesses work well and in what contexts, the various tricks for reining in token usage, and so forth and so on. Of course, like *everyone else* in the software industry, I know I still have plenty to learn. But that's what makes it fun.

If you're an ambitious maker with well-honed software engineering skills and intuition built on years of experience, this year's breed of LLMs feel truly magical. You can now move incredibly fast. You can build projects as a solo developer that previously required a small team of engineers to pull off. And, with discipline, you can build them *well*. The entire apparatus is—I hate to admit—rather addictive for someone like me. I haven't felt this excited about making software in a long time. Projects that have languished for years as an inkling of an idea, buried deep in some Obsidian note, suddenly feel achievable. Some of them I've already built. It is, perhaps, the golden age of the side project ... or is it?

---

I'm not the only one to notice the bill coming coming due. I suspect there are many more out there [amassing lists of genuinely impressive things built with AI and then abandoned](https://thoughts.hmmz.org/2026-05-31.html); almost none of them useful, none of them maintained. Those of us on side-project frenzies are rapidly discovering that attention is not just all you need; it is all you *have.* It's both one thing AI can't generate for us, and one thing it is most eager to spend on our behalf.

On evenings when I had the opportunity to choose between spending a precious few hours making software, or devote that same time to the much slower, harder work of thinking and writing, I always chose building. This was a mistake. I can feel the atrophy of my (already weak) writing muscles as I type these very words. Everything is coming out slower, more stilted. And if my writing has declined, so too has my thinking. I need to write to think.

So, to borrow a phrase from [Gordon Brander](https://newsletter.squishy.computer), I'm solving this problem by [dropping to log level](https://newsletter.squishy.computer/p/dropping-to-log-level). That is, I am returning to another practice that has always served me well at times of learning and change: blogging. Fewer long-form essays, more raw, less-polished work. 

I am not going to stop building with AI. This moment that calls for experimentation and hands on keyboard. But I *am* going to be more deliberate about what I build, and how much. I learned my lesson: that the maximum number of side-projects I'm willing to take on at any given time, even with AI, is exactly one. This is what it take to go deep enough to build anything I'll actually care about. The rest of that attention, I'm spending here.

It’s time to get back to blogging.

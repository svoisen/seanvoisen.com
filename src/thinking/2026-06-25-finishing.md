title: 'Finishing'
description: 'On perseverance and care as anti-slop.'

Some of the best advice I ever received came years ago from a coworker named Joe. 

Joe always had a few side-projects going on—weird and wonderful mashups of electronics, software, and fabrication of just about every kind: welding, woodwork, 3D printing, you name it. His projects were the kinds of things you might catch at your local Maker Faire, or-if you're that kind of person-Burning Man. What I admired most about Joe wasn't just his initiative and creativity—it was that he always seemed to finish everything he started. He didn't just build things; he built, documented, and showcased them, even when the project became a slog and the sparkle of enjoyment had long since faded and he had three other things on the back burner. Joe traced his resolve back to something his grandfather once told him: That the true test of character is not whether you start something, but whether you can see it through to the end. Perseverance comes from a willingness to *care*.

I've been returning to this advice a lot lately. With AI at your fingertips, it's never been easier to start a project. And, conversely, never harder to finish. An idea for a new app pops into your head? "I'll have Claude build it." Want to spin up a new website? Easy! Bring out the bots. The distance from idea to execution is so short—with execution shoved off into a distant background thread—that you can now theoretically start 50 or more projects at once. We're in, [as I noted before](/thinking/2026-06-01-dropping-to-blog-level/), the "golden age of the side-project." We can now "vibe" all the things.

Of course, "vibing" is infamous for resulting in unfinished work. Hard drives and servers everywhere littered with the detritus of half-thought-through, quickly abandoned projects. Things that *look* finished on the surface, but are riddled with holes and rough edges that require more work, more iteration, more polish. To "vibe" is to—by definition—not care, to adopt a "good enough" attitude. Which is why it's become such a pejorative. It creates slop.

The only countermeasure to slop, then, is the willingness to care, to meter out your attention to the few things that matter most, to not just start a bunch of things, but to start only a few, and finish them ... completely.
